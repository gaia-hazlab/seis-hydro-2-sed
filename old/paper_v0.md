# Transect-scale seismic monitoring of bedload transport on Mt. Rainier's glacial rivers during the December 2025 atmospheric-river floods

*Working manuscript — Denolle et al., target: JGR: Earth Surface (alt: ESurf / Seismica). Draft scaffold, June 2026.*

## Abstract ⬜
~200 words. Hook: Dec-2025 atmospheric rivers drove record floods + debris flows off Mt. Rainier. We use near-channel seismic stations along the Puyallup/Carbon glacial rivers, paired with USGS gages, to show that high-frequency seismic power scales super-linearly with discharge (b≈2) and steepens with frequency above the turbulent-flow baseline — a transect-scale bedload signal — with event hysteresis recording sediment-supply dynamics. First bedload seismology on Cascades glacial rivers and first tied to a named Pacific atmospheric-river event.

## 1. Introduction ⬜
- Bedload is hard to measure directly; seismic noise offers continuous, non-contact monitoring (Burtin 2008; Tsai 2012; Gimbert 2014; Cook & Dietze 2022).
- Two-source framework: turbulent flow (low f, ~linear in Q) vs bedload impacts (high f, nonlinear/threshold, ∝ q_b·D³).
- Sediment hazard context: Mt. Rainier aggradation, lahar legacy, Puyallup→Puget Sound connectivity (Czuba 2011/2012; Anderson 2025).
- The Dec-2025 AR floods: a natural experiment (confirmed record/near-record floods; debris flows; park closure).
- 💬 Gap statement (from LITERATURE.md §7): lead with **combination** — transect scale + Cascades glacial rivers + named AR event. Do **not** claim the frequency-scaling diagnostic as novel (Bakker 2020).
- Objectives: (i) field-calibrate the per-station water baseline; (ii) map β(f) and hysteresis along a mountain-to-sea transect; (iii) interpret deviations as bedload-supply signals.

## 2. Setting & event ⬛
- Puyallup River system, Mt. Rainier WA; glacial source (Puyallup/Tahoma/Carbon glaciers) → lowland → Commencement Bay (Puget Sound), ~54 km.
- Stations (Table 1): upper broadband cluster CC.PR01–04, STYX, VOIT, TRON, B941; lowland urban accelerometers UW.PAYL, GS.TFD, UW.BRPT, UW.UPS. Control: UW.BHW (Snohomish).
- Gages: 12092000 Electron, 12094000 Carbon/Fairfax, 12093500 Orting, 12096500 Alderton, 12101500 Puyallup; 12082500 Nisqually.
- Event: two atmospheric rivers ~Dec 8–12 2025; Puyallup-nr-Electron peak ≈323 m³/s at 2025-12-09 ~03:30 UTC; Carbon highest in 19 yr; statewide emergency.

## 3. Data & Methods 🟦
### 3.1 Seismic proxy
- FDSN waveforms, whole-UTC-day blocks; **strict** instrument-response removal to ground velocity (no raw-counts fallback — Tier-1 fix); RSS over Z/N/E; Welch PSD integrated over band → P_band(t), 10-min windows / 5-min step.
- Bands: flow {0.5–2,1–5,2–8}; bedload {5–15,10–30,30–60} Hz.
- Cleaning: USGS-event masking (M≥3.5, 500 km, ±20 min); STA/LTA impulse clipping restricted to triggered windows (Tier-1 fix); rolling-MAD despike.
### 3.2 Discharge alignment
- USGS IV discharge; constant-lag scan with **high-pass detrending** before correlation and physical sign constraint (Tier-1 fix; replaces spurious −18 h lag).
### 3.3 Scaling & hysteresis
- Robust log–log fit P ∝ Q^b (OLS + Theil–Sen, bootstrap 95% CI); per-band b(f_c).
- Water baseline b≈0.9–1.4 (Gimbert 2014) shaded as reference; **β_excess = b − 1.4**.
- Lawler (1989) hysteresis index per event (±3 d around peak); sense ⇒ supply interpretation.
- 💬 Reproducibility: `config/*.yaml`, `workflows/`, `src/riverseis/`; figures regenerated by `workflows/02_make_figures.py`.

## 4. Results 🟦  (numbers from `paper/figures/scaling_table.csv`; bug-fixed pipeline)
- **R1 — frequency-dependent steepening (Fig 2, 3).** CC.PR03 (clean run): b = **1.54 [1.53,1.56]** (2–8 Hz, r=0.94) → **1.66 [1.64,1.67]** (5–15 Hz, r=0.94). Slope sits modestly above the turbulence baseline and rises with frequency. 💬 The Tier-1 fixes (strict response removal, windowed clipping, detrended lag; see REVIEW_2026.md) **reduced** an earlier, contamination-inflated estimate (b≈2.0) and shifted the lag from −18 h → −6 h — a more conservative, defensible signal. Full clean β(f) across 5–15/10–30/30–60 Hz pending an all-band export.
- **R2 — negative control (Fig 2).** UW.BHW (lowland Snohomish): b=0.54 (0.5–2 Hz), **b=0.07, r=0.06** (10–30 Hz) — no high-frequency discharge coupling ⇒ the PR03 high-f response is not a generic flow artifact. 💬 Control still uses pre-fix proxy; re-run for consistency.
- **R3 — hysteresis (Fig 4).** Event Lawler index is small at all upper stations (|HI|≲0.06), i.e. near-single-valued P–Q during this flood; no strong clockwise/counter-clockwise loop survives cleaning. 💬 The batch's −6 h lag at co-located PR03 hints at a rising-limb lead but is not corroborated by HI — treat as unresolved pending per-event analysis.
- **R4 — transect decay (Fig 2).** At 5–15 Hz the bedload-band exponent decays from the glacial source downstream/with distance from the channel: **PR03 (0.19 km) b=1.66, PR02 (1.9 km) b=1.67, PR01 (0.71 km) b=1.19, TRON (RM≈20, 5.2 km) b=0.61 (r=0.55)**; lowland control UW.BHW b=0.07. 💬 **Confound:** TRON's 5.2 km standoff implies strong high-frequency attenuation (the e^(−2πfr/vQ) term), so the downstream weakening is partly a distance-from-channel effect, not solely reduced transport — this is the central interpretive caveat for the mountain-to-sea claim. Full per-station β(f) lines await the all-band re-run (only PR03 currently has two river bands).

## 5. Discussion ⬜
- Interpretation: super-linear high-f scaling ⇒ threshold-controlled bedload (q_b ∝ (τ*−τ*_c)^1.5) + D³ grain leverage during the flood/debris-flow sediment pulse.
- 💬 **Defense vs Nativ et al. (2025):** static-boulder turbulence could raise high-f power; we argue against via (a) the temporal/event hysteresis, (b) the lowland null result, (c) frequency-dependent steepening tracking the flood, not a fixed bed.
- 💬 **Defense vs Roth et al. (2017):** roughness-change hysteresis; tie HI sense to the debris-flow supply chronology, not stage alone.
- 💬 **Reconcile baseline:** we adopt an empirical per-station water baseline; note Gimbert's ~1.4 vs our measured low band.
- Connectivity: relate to Czuba (2012) aggradation & pulse transit times; what a transect adds over single-reach arrays (Roth 2014; Schmandt 2017; Antoniazza 2023).
- Limitations: no absolute flux inversion (proxy only); downstream coverage is urban accelerometers; single event.
